# Preserving Unknown Rare Subpopulations in Single-Cell Batch Integration

## Batch Integration Is a Foundational Step in Single-Cell Analysis

Batch integration in single-cell RNA sequencing is an almost unavoidable step in multi-sample and multi-source single-cell studies. Data generated by different laboratories, sequencing platforms, or time points contain systematic technical variation, known as batch effects. Without correction, downstream clustering and annotation may be driven by technical noise rather than biological signal. Over the past decade, many integration methods have been developed, including Harmony, scVI, Scanorama, Seurat, fastMNN, BBKNN, LIGER, scGen, scANVI, and others. In 2022, Luecken et al. published the scIB benchmark in Nature Methods, systematically evaluating 16 methods across 85 batches and more than 1.2 million cells, establishing the most authoritative evaluation framework in the field.

## Rare Subpopulations Being Eliminated During Integration Is a Long-Standing Concern

The field recognized early on that batch integration may remove real biological differences while eliminating technical variation. This issue is known as overcorrection, and it is the central tension in integration: the more thoroughly batch effects are removed, the greater the risk of losing biological signal.

Rare subpopulations are the most vulnerable part of this tension. The reason is straightforward: rare subpopulations contain few cells and occupy a small volume in embedding space. When integration algorithms align batches, these cells can easily be pushed into nearby larger populations, causing them to disappear as independent groups. Multiple high-impact papers have explicitly documented this phenomenon:

- **scDML** (2023, Nature Communications) states at the outset that most existing algorithms remove batch effects before clustering, and that this workflow may lose rare cell types. The core value proposition of scDML is preserving rare subpopulations. In human pancreas data, only scDML successfully separated rare types such as activated stellate, epsilon, and schwann cells into independent clusters; in macaque retina data, only scDML identified the fine-grained OFFx subtype.

- **scDREAMER** (2023, Nature Communications) found in lung atlas integration that only scDREAMER, scVI, and iMAP preserved rare ionocyte cells as an independent cluster, while other methods, including Harmony, Seurat, and INSCT, mixed them with other types. scDML also showed cell-type merging on this dataset.

- **SCIDRL** (2022, Nucleic Acids Research) was specifically optimized for two scenarios: effective integration of rare subpopulations shared across batches, and preservation of batch-specific rare subpopulations.

- **scCRAFT** (2025, Communications Biology) designed a dual-resolution topology-preserving mechanism, with the core motivation of resisting overcorrection caused by domain-adaptation loss and protecting cellular heterogeneity.

- **STACAS** (2024, Nature Communications) explicitly noted that integration often leads to overcorrection and loss of biological variation, and proposed using prior cell-type information to guide integration.

- **CellANOVA** (2024, Nature Biotechnology) directly demonstrated that current single-cell data integration paradigms are overly aggressive and remove meaningful biological variation, and proposed a signal-recovery framework to remedy signals lost after integration.

- **RBET** (2025, Communications Biology) pointed out that there is no simple metric that can evaluate batch-correction performance in a way that is sensitive to overcorrection. Overcorrection can erase real biological variation and lead to incorrect biological discoveries.

- **scIB-E** (2025, Genome Biology) found that the scIB framework has fundamental limitations in capturing the preservation of biological variation within cell types, and proposed expanded metrics.

- A **Nature Computational Science review** (February 2026) summarized that despite the development of many batch-correction algorithms, they still frequently struggle between overcorrection and undercorrection.

- The **latest semi-supervised integration benchmark** (2025, PLOS Computational Biology) found that even the best semi-supervised methods rapidly degrade when labels contain structural defects, falling below the performance of basic unsupervised methods.

This is not a marginal issue. It is the central trade-off in the integration field and has been repeatedly discussed in top-tier publications for at least five years.

## But the Problem Has Still Not Been Truly Solved

All of the work above, whether algorithms claiming to protect rare subpopulations (scDML, SCIDRL, scCRAFT), post-processing frameworks attempting to recover lost signals (CellANOVA), or benchmarks proposing new evaluation metrics (scIB, scIB-E, RBET), shares one common premise:

**They evaluate the preservation of known cell types.**

scDML verifies that it can preserve epsilon cells because epsilon cells already exist in the annotation. scDREAMER verifies that it can preserve ionocytes because ionocytes already exist in the annotation. scIB uses ARI, NMI, and ASW to measure agreement between clustering and annotation, with annotation serving as the ground truth. CellANOVA recovers known differences between known conditions. RBET relies on reference datasets to evaluate overcorrection.

**No method can detect whether a rare subpopulation absent from the annotation has been eliminated during integration.**

If a rare subpopulation had never been annotated before integration, perhaps hidden at the edge of a large population and only identifiable with sufficient statistical power after enough batches are combined, all existing evaluation frameworks are blind to it. If it is eliminated, no metric decreases; if it is preserved, no metric increases. It does not exist within the evaluation system.

## What This Means

If integration systematically eliminates unknown rare subpopulations, then all downstream discoveries made from integrated data, including differential expression analysis, cell-cell communication inference, trajectory inference, and disease association, rest on an untested assumption:

**Integration did not discard anything important.**

This assumption has never been validated, because validating it requires knowing what was lost in advance. But if you already know it, it is no longer "unknown."

## Problem Definition

**In single-cell batch integration, there is no method capable of detecting or protecting previously unknown rare subpopulations.**

This problem has been continuously recognized by the field for at least five years. Many algorithms and evaluation frameworks have been proposed, but all of them remain limited to known cell types. For unknown rare subpopulations, the field has neither detection methods nor protection mechanisms.

## Disciplines Required to Solve This Problem

Machine learning, mathematics, computational biology, immunology, data science, cancer biology, biological science, and philosophy.

## What Counts as Solving This Problem

This problem should be considered solved only when the following five criteria are met:

**1. Detection**: There is an evaluation framework that can quantitatively detect whether integration has eliminated a previously unknown rare subpopulation without relying on external labels.

**2. Protection**: There is an integration algorithm or integration strategy that is shown under this evaluation framework to significantly reduce the loss rate of unknown rare subpopulations, without sacrificing batch-correction performance for major cell types.

**3. Scalability**: The framework and algorithm can handle real-world-scale datasets. The reference standard is the pan-cancer immune atlas constructed by Kang et al. (2024, Nature Communications), which integrates single-cell data from 104 papers, 943 patients, 30 cancer types, and 4.9 million cells. This is one of the largest integrated datasets in the field. Any method claiming to solve rare-subpopulation preservation is not practically meaningful if it cannot operate at this scale.

**4. Computational Validation**: Known rare cell types in published datasets, such as pancreatic epsilon cells and lung ionocytes, are used as ground truth to verify that the framework can indeed detect the loss of these known rare subpopulations during integration. This validation capacity already exists in current benchmark systems and can be directly reused.

**5. Biological Validation**: At least one previously unknown rare subpopulation detected by the framework as "eliminated during integration" is confirmed through independent wet-lab experiments to truly exist and have biological function. This is the only way to fundamentally demonstrate that the framework is detecting real biological signal rather than computational artifacts.
